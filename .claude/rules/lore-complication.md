# Lore complication — set a rule, then invert it

Depth comes from contradiction, not accumulation. Whenever you establish a rule, expectation, or
clean alignment, find the grounded exception that breaks it. The inversion is what makes a situation
morally interesting and stops the world reading as a tidy faction chart.

## The move

1. **State the rule / expectation.** "The Vexlings are mindless killers." "Gorathi priests want
   Tamadrezan dwarf culture erased."
2. **Invert it with a grounded exception** — a named faction, person, or concrete reason that cuts
   against the rule. "One Vexling brood was raised up around a priest of mercy." "A faction inside
   the church is successfully converting Drasnian dwarves, who share the faith but refuse to be the
   state's instrument of intimidation."
3. **Leave the tension unresolved.** The rule and its inversion are both true at once. That
   contradiction is the texture — and it makes it unclear who the real villains are.

## Why

- Clean rules read as AI worldbuilding. A rule with a living exception reads as a real society.
- Moral ambiguity follows automatically: if the "monsters" have a mercy-cult and the "oppressors"
  have principled dissenters, no one sorts neatly into good/bad. That uncertainty is the goal.
- Every inversion is a hook — a faction, a person, or a conflict an author or GM can pull on.

## Discipline

- The exception must be **grounded and named**, never hand-waved. "Some of them are different" is
  not an inversion; a named brood/faction/person with a concrete cause is. Two-way link it like any
  other lore (prose backlink + edge).
- **Complicate, don't delete.** The exception is the rare, costly anomaly — the Vexlings are still
  overwhelmingly violent; the mercy-brood is hunted by its own kind.
- **Invert recursively** where you want real depth: an inversion can itself be inverted.
- This is a depth tool for the **load-bearing rules of a cluster**, not a mandate for every
  footnote entity.
