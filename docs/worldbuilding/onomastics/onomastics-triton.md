# Triton Onomastics

> **sparse sample — extrapolate conservatively**

**Author anchor:** Hard-stop bookends, liquid bridge, nasal close — martial and terse; classical maritime register  
**Source pool (frozen — do not copy verbatim):** Triton (race name; sole confirmed sample)

## Sound palette
- The race name supplies the entire skeleton: **T-R-i-t-on** — hard T opener, liquid r bridge, short i vowel, hard T interior, nasal -on close
- Consonants that recur: hard stops (t, d, k) for openers and interiors; liquids (r, l) as bridges; nasal or hard-stop terminals (-on, -an, -orn, -ank)
- Vowel behavior: short and compressed (i, o, a); no open diphthong runs; names should be command-capable — one breath, clear
- Forbidden / off-style: soft fricatives (z, v) as openers; long open vowel sequences; anything that reads soft, fae, or flowing — triton warrior culture implies terseness

## Morphemes & affixes
- Tr-/T- opener: derived from the race name; the hard-T-plus-liquid cluster is the strongest confirmed signal
- -on/-an/-orn terminal: nasal close from the race name; variants with harder stops (-ank, -ard) possible for place names
- Short interior: 1-syllable bridge between opener and terminal (Tri = one bridge syllable); total name length 2–3 syllables
- The doubling of the T sound (Tri-ton = T...T) suggests hard consonant repetition is idiomatic

## Naming customs
- 2–3 syllables; monosyllables read as rank/title abbreviations rather than given names
- Hard T or D opener is near-mandatory — reflects martial tradition; names you can shout across an underwater arena
- Liquid (r, l) bridge between opener and terminal is the standard structure
- Place names (underwater cities) likely follow the same skeleton as personal names, possibly extended to 3 syllables
- The lone confirmed sample "Triton" is itself the race identity name — personal names probably vary the vowel and terminal while keeping the hard-opener pattern
