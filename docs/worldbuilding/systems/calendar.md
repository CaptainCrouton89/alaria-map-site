# Calendar

Invariant: SD/BSD is the sole civil calendar. Calendar zero (0 SD) is the threshold the surviving calendars fixed just after the World Fire of ~10 BSD; SD counts forward from it and BSD backward. Now is 3376 SD. The Great Cycle (its halves marked F and B) is a cyclical astronomical position on a 25,300-year wheel of Aurus/Nydus drift and lunar alignment — observed, not used to date events. It does not convert to civil dates and has no year zero. The Seventh Dawn is the current era.

There is one calendar for history and one wheel for the sky, and they do not exchange dates.

## The civil calendar

SD and BSD are the historical reckoning. Every dated event in the codex uses them. SD counts forward from calendar zero, the threshold the surviving calendars fixed just after the World Fire (the leyline rupture of roughly 10 BSD), and BSD counts backward from that same threshold. The present is 3376 SD. A date written 9,950 BSD fell 9,950 years before calendar zero (0 SD); a date written 312 SD fell 312 years after it.

The Seventh Dawn is the current era, the most recent named period of the third eon. 0 SD sits at calendar zero, the threshold the surviving calendars fixed in the World Fire's immediate aftermath (the burning itself fell about a decade earlier, ~10 BSD). The two reckonings meet at that threshold, an exact seam rather than a conversion.

## The Great Cycle

The Great Cycle is the slow astronomical clock of the sky, not a second count of history. It runs from 0 to 25,300 and back down again, one full turn spanning 25,300 years (roughly 14,000 Earth years). What it measures is a position, not an elapsed total: where the world sits on the wheel of Aurus and Nydus's drift toward and away from the Material boundary, and the lunar alignments that drift produces. The drift is what the moons resonate with, so the two suns and the two moons share one long rhythm, and the Great Cycle is the dial that reads it.

Observatories mark that position with F and B — F for the ascending half of the wheel, B for the descending. A reading of F or B names a place on the dial. It does not name a year. Two eras separated by a full 25,300-year turn read identically on it, which is exactly why it cannot date anything: the cycle returns, and history does not. Because it marks a recurring position rather than a running total, it has no year zero and no offset that would tie it to SD/BSD. Omen-readers, ritual astronomers, and the temples that time their rites to the sky use the Great Cycle; chroniclers use SD/BSD. No event in the codex is dated in F/B, and none should be.

An earlier framing held that any SD date could be converted into a Great Cycle position through the cycle-position of the Seventh Dawn's start. That model is retracted. A cyclical position carries no linear date inside it, so there is nothing to convert; the two systems answer different questions and never trade values.

The two scales meet inside the Lost Ages. Everything deeper than that is given in round years before present, because nothing survives from those spans precise enough to date in civil years. From the Lost Ages forward the BSD record takes over, and a BSD year converts to years before present by adding the 3,376 years of the Seventh Dawn — so the Shattering at 10,209 BSD fell roughly 13,500 years ago, not 10,209. The old habit of reading a BSD figure as if it were a years-ago figure is the error that once placed the Shattering three thousand years too late.

## The shape of the civil year

The civil year runs 200 days, split into eight quarters of 25 days each. It is a treaty, not a measurement. The Solar Accord — a pact among the major prayer-coalitions — partitioned Bryn's annual path into a standardized cycle of four warm quarters and four cool ones; before it, and outside its signatory regions, seasons lasted as long as a coalition could hold the sun on a given trail. There are no solstices to anchor the year, because the world has no axial tilt. New year falls on the date the Accord designated, a political convention. The full structure of seasons, quarters, and their politics lives in `seasons.md` and in `plane-time-and-seasons.md`; the calendar layer is below.

### The week

The week is 11 days, set by the cycle of the bright moon Auris, beginning at new moon and reaching full moon mid-week. The days are named: Cautious (new moon, the week's most dangerous night), Wakeday, Patrons' Day, Moil, Marketday, Merrynight (full moon), Brightlove, Lazyday, Farewell, Gather, and Fireday (waning crescent, the last light before the dark return to Cautious).

### The two moons and their holidays

Two moons cross the sky. Auris runs an 11-day cycle and marks the week. Nyxara runs a 23-day cycle and marks a deeper magical tide. Because 11 and 23 share no common factor, the two faces coincide only every 253 days — slightly longer than the 200-day year. When both are full at once the night is Brightnight; when both are dark it is Hollownight. Both are major holidays. The 253-day return of Brightnight, the monthly turn of Nyxara, and the weekly tide of Auris are the lunar holidays, kept most closely by moon elves and the magic-centered cultures, for whom the moons' alignment is the rhythm of magic.

### Quarters and seasonal holidays

The eight quarters carry the solar holidays, kept most closely by commonfolk and farmers, for whom the calendar's turn is the rhythm of nature. The quarters and the holidays that fall on them are named in `plane-time-and-seasons.md`; that file also carries a worked full-date example and the named seasonal turns.

A full civil date names the weekday, the day of the quarter, and the SD or BSD year — for example, Marketday, the 14th of Highsun, 3376 SD.
