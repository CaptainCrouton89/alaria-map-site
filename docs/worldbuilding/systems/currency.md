# Money and credit

Invariant: Alaria has no single currency. The developed core reckons money in iron — the Aldriktch standard, a token coinage minted and underwritten by the Adron banking houses and kept on the books of the six Aldriktch Trade Alliance members and the Tarkhon Empire. The southern trade runs on a different idea of money entirely: weighed fine silver, no central mint, every port a scale. The Dalizi Confederation keeps no coin at all. What ties the two metal standards to one number is the Royal Adrak Bank, the only house large enough to publish an exchange rate that sticks, so that a southern mark of silver and a northern iron reckoning meet at a price Adron sets. Above all coin sit two assets nobody spends: titan bone, the reserve of last resort, and red gold, the store of value too useful to circulate.

This file is the money and credit companion to `economy.md`, which owns goods, tolls, and the commodity map. The chokepoint tolls and commodity trades that file describes are denominated, settled, and financed here; this file does not restate them. It documents the metal standards, the reserve assets, and the credit institutions, and defers the prayer-economy — which is a life-tithe, not coin — to `daemon-economics.md`.

The regional split is deliberate. A merchant crossing from the Middle Sea to the Free Isles changes more than the coins in the purse: the very idea of what money is changes under them, from a number reckoned in the cheapest metal to a weight of silver on a balance. The currencies do not agree because the regions that issue them never had to.

## The Aldriktch standard

The Middle Sea reckons in iron. Not because anyone settles a debt in iron bars, but because iron is the unit of account — the number every price is quoted in, the way a modern ledger quotes a sum it never holds as physical metal. A loaf is one or two iron. A warhorse is a thousand. The choice was made on purpose by the Adron houses that underwrite the standard, and the logic is the logic of a banker: a unit of account anchored to the cheapest honest metal is a unit nobody can hoard and nobody can corner. Gold can be cornered. The smith's iron cannot.

The ladder climbs by tens, each rung a different metal:

- Iron piece — one. Struck in iron. Street food, candles, a night in a bad room, a soldier's daily bread.
- Obol — ten. A heavier bronze coin. A day's wage, or a cheap blade.
- Lire — one hundred. Silver. A good weapon, a comfortable lodging, a season of a skilled hand's work.
- Ducat — one thousand. A gold-bearing coin. A warhorse. The deed to a property.
- Crown — ten thousand. Platinum-bearing. A ship, a castle's repair, an army in the field for a season.

The standard is loose, which is the word `economy.md` uses for it and the right one. The iron reckoning is near-universal, but the coins that carry it drift. A Bestacian lire and a Watari lire are both a hundred iron on paper; on a scale they are not quite the same silver, and a careful factor weighs both. What holds the system together is not enforcement but redemption — the Royal Adrak Bank stands behind the reckoning, publishes the rates the others quote against, and will exchange the standard's coin for its weight when pressed. The Alliance members keep their books in iron-reckoned Adrak even where they mint their own faces onto the metal. So does Tarkhon, whose customs houses tax the strait in a coin the empire did not issue, because the empire would rather collect a reckoning everyone already trusts than persuade the world to trust its own.

## The Crimson Crown and the red-platinum question

Above the Crown sits one more denomination, and it is barely a coin. The Crimson Crown is struck from red platinum — rarer than the Crown's ordinary platinum by a wide margin, worth more than ten thousand iron and quoted at no fixed multiple at all, because so few exist that each one trades like a small kingdom changing hands. Most are not spent. They are vaulted, several of them in the Bank of Infindior, and they surface to settle the kind of balance that ends a war or buys a throne.

Red platinum is not red gold, and the two must not be confused. Red gold is Adron's mined metal, already canon: a reddish gold that never tarnishes and turns aside elemental Kethic the way it turns aside light, which is why it counts among the magical materials and not the merely precious ones. Red platinum is a different element — a platinum-group metal that takes a red cast, recovered in trace amounts from the same eastern ranges that give Adron its gold, and a hundred times scarcer. It does not turn Kethic. That single difference is how a true Crimson Crown is told from a forgery: a counterfeit struck in red gold scatters a Kethic test off its face, while real red platinum lets the working pass clean through. A coin that should be the most valuable object in the room and refuses to turn a flame is the genuine article. The one that turns it is a fake worth a fortune less.

## The southern reckoning

South of the Middle Sea, money is not a coin. It is a weight of silver.

Kyagos, the Aboyinzu trade, the Shackland slave markets of South Rimihuica, and the Free Isles of Upoceax all run on the same standard, and it is genuinely a different standard, not a southern dialect of the northern one. There is no unit of account reckoned in iron. There is no central mint. There is fine silver, assayed and weighed, and the trade unit is the mark — a standard weight of it — with cut silver below that for small dealings and, the deeper inland a trader goes, salt and bolts of cloth for the smallest. The Free Isles families each cast and stamp their own marks; a Valdrossi mark and a Gattorini mark are cut to the same weight and a Kokotintin moneychanger still bites both. On Kyagos the silver is worn openly and scarred like its bearers. In the Shacklands the legions are paid in marks and the slavers settle in them, and the great war-creditor of the south lends them by the chestful.

The two standards are different kinds of money — token versus weight, a number versus a balance — and they still meet at one price, because the Royal Adrak Bank publishes a silver-to-iron cross-rate and the southern houses quote against it. Not from obligation. From arithmetic: Adron is the only house heavy enough to make a rate hold, so the south reckons its weighed silver against the north's iron at a number set in Adrak, even where no Adron coin is anywhere on the table. The southern merchant who has never seen an iron piece still trades against one every day.

## The Dalizi exception

The Dalizi Confederation keeps no coin, mints no metal, and reckons its wealth in cattle.

The unit of account is a head of cattle — ng'ombe — and like the north's iron piece it is mostly notional: a debt is named in cattle that never walk anywhere. Settlement happens in bars of lake salt and stamped lengths of cloth, issued by the member states, valued against the cattle reckoning. The rates are set each year by the Mwali at whichever city holds the rotating seat, which puts the confederation's money in the hands of whoever is hosting — and the host shades the rate toward its own salt and its own cloth, every year, a grievance as old as kusafirisha kiti and as unprovable as a herd count. Because the rate is reckoned in cattle, a hard season of raiding moves the whole confederation's money at once: a bloc that loses its herds watches its salt and cloth inflate against a unit it can no longer field. The seat-cycle is a money-cycle, and the two grievances feed each other.

The cattle reckoning barely crosses the confederation's border. A Dalizi trader carrying goods to the lake ports converts at the water's edge into southern silver marks, at a rate that runs, in the end, back to Adron.

## The reserve assets

Two things sit above all coin, and neither one is money in the sense of changing hands at a market.

Titan bone is the reserve of last resort, and the whole financial layer is weighed against it. It is the one store of value that cannot be minted, cannot be inflated, and does not tarnish — the supply is fixed, roughly nineteen parts in twenty held by Deo Esari, and shrinking year over year as the veins run dry. The great houses settle their largest balances and back their heaviest loans against bone, because a reserve nobody can manufacture is the only reserve that holds when every coin is suspect. The material itself, its monopoly, and its depletion belong to `economy.md` and `magic-titan-blood-bones.md`; what matters here is its role as the floor under the credit system.

Red gold is the supra-luxury — value held precisely because it is too valuable and too useful to spend. A red-gold shield is a fortune worn rather than banked, and the Kethic it turns aside makes it worth more to a duelist or a strongroom than its weight in any coin. It is wealth a house keeps. It is not wealth a house circulates.

## The credit layer

The metal would sit inert without the houses that lend it, and those houses are what turn coin into a working financial system.

Adron is the center of it. The Royal Adrak Bank mints and underwrites the Aldriktch standard, publishes the cross-rate that binds north to south, lends at interest from the depth of its counting-houses, and writes most of the Middle Sea's war-loans and the underwriting on most of the continent's sky vessels. Mariseni's house stands first among the banking houses that govern the kingdom, and half the Middle Sea owes its ledgers. The distance from the Tarkhon chokepoint is what frees Adron to be everyone's creditor rather than anyone's rival, a point `economy.md` and the Adron entry both carry.

In the south the same function wears a grimmer face. Belmonte runs the oldest banking house in Azantir and finances Gorath's wars on advances secured against ground the empire has not taken yet — the southern mirror of Adron's war-loans, except that where Adron's largest paper is backed by bone, Belmonte's is backed by conquests not yet made. A campaign that wins repays its bond. A campaign that stalls leaves a hole only the next campaign can fill, which is why the man who holds the empire's debt needs the war never to end.

The Bank of Infindior is the system's vault of last resort, and a stranger thing than any counting-house. It is a place where time does not pass, so a deposit neither ages nor decays nor can be reached by anyone but its depositor, and the great houses use it to hold titan-bone reserves, the few Crimson Crowns, and fortunes that other parties would very much like to freeze or seize. Its door stands in Kyorda, on the Denrik Sea within reach of the Adron houses. The custody it offers is also a weapon: a thing put beyond time is a thing put beyond a creditor, a court, or an heir, and the trade that runs through Infindior is at least as much about denying access as granting it. The plane carries the mechanics.

Two daemons stand over the commerce itself. Bylzar — propagation, continuity, gold — is the god of the merchant house that survives across centuries, prayed to by the banker who wants the dynasty to endure. Foedros — contract, the held signature — is the god of the single enforceable deal, prayed to at the moment the seal hits the wax and at no other. The financial layer keeps a continuity-god for the house and a contract-god for the document, and a great many notaries pay both without seeing any conflict between the long arc and the next delivery. The daemon files carry the cults and their quarrels.
