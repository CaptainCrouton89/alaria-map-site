# Topic H — Strategic shallowness, referential depth

The depth-is-referential thesis in `plan/high-level.md` claims a one-sentence gesture outperforms a paragraph of description. This doc surveys the named precedents for that claim, the documented failure modes when it gets ignored, and the specific Foundations-stage moves that operationalize it for Alaria's 3,541-to-10,000 corpus growth.

## Precedents

### Tolkien's deliberate horizons

The clearest articulation of the technique is Tolkien's own. Letter 247 (Sept 1963, to Colonel Worskett) names the mechanism: "Part of the attraction of the L. R. is, I think, due to the glimpses of a large history in the background: an attraction like that of viewing far off an unvisited island, or seeing the towers of a distant city gleaming in a sunlit mist. To go there is to destroy the magic, unless new unattainable vistas are again revealed" (Carpenter ed., *Letters*, 1981, Letter 247). Letter 131 (Waldman, c. 1951) names the design intent prospectively: "I would draw some of the great tales in fullness, and leave many only placed in the scheme, and sketched." Letter 144 (Mitchison, April 1954) names the working principle: "it is good that there should be a lot of things unexplained...and I have perhaps from this point of view erred in trying to explain too much."

The gesture itself is concrete. Far Harad exists in one clause of *The Return of the King* (Book V Ch. VI): "out of Far Harad black men like half-trolls with white eyes and red tongues." That is the totality of Far Harad in the published text. Khand and Variags get the same treatment.

Tom Shippey (*The Road to Middle-earth*, 1982) named the technique "impression of depth," locating it in casual mention of background readers never see — "tantalising glimpses" into a backstory "complete" but withheld. Michael Drout et al. (*Tolkien Studies* vol. 4, 2007) decomposed it into four mechanisms: scale of unwritten background, casual incomplete mention as if taken for granted, multiple accounts with gaps and contradictions, and multiple writing styles. Peter Grybauskas (*A Sense of Tales Untold*, Kent State UP, 2021) frames the whole technique as restraint, citing Tolkien's letter to Christopher (30 Jan 1945, *Letters* p. 110): "A story must be told or there'll be no story, yet it is the untold stories that are most moving."

### Glorantha's Pamaltela

Greg Stafford ran the same model across an entire continent. The 1988 Avalon Hill *Genertela: Crucible of the Hero Wars* boxed set shipped with a 100-page Genertela Book; the planned companion Pamaltela Book was cut for size and did not appear until 1998 as *The Missing Lands*, a 114-page spiral-bound "work in progress" in a small print run. *The Guide to Glorantha* (Stafford & Richard, Chaosium, 2014) devotes ~300 of ~800 pages to Genertela; Pamaltela receives a fraction in Vol. 2 across seven regions. Fonrit, the most-documented Pamaltela region, has roughly 8 source citations across the bibliography; Dragon Pass has more than 20. *Tales of the Reaching Moon* dedicated one issue (#11, 1994) to all of Pamaltela; Prax got two issues, Sartar and the Lunars each got their own.

Specific entries demonstrate the depth ratio. Jolar in *The Guide*: "Nomadic Agimori and other peoples roam these wide plains, herding cattle and hunting. The Kresh Empire in the east prompted the organization of the Arbennan Kingdom, a confederation of tribes." Kothar: "The Kresh nomads, who ride upon wagons of colossal size, originally came from here. With cities on wheels, they rumble about, exacting food as tribute." Tarien: "A wide plain with relatively few humans, but sometimes thick with lizard men and their great beasts." Genertela's Dragon Pass receives chapter-length treatment with political maps, population data, hero-wars prophecies, named figures, and per-sub-region events (citations indexed at Well of Daliath, *Guide to Glorantha* contents).

The Pamaltela page on Well of Daliath retroactively canonizes the gap: "Several regions of Pamaltela are shown on the map, but not treated in the text. These are hidden lands, unknown to all outsiders." Jeff Richard's commentary defends Pamaltela's apparent stasis as a reader-side issue, not an authoring gap: "The Doraddi...seem timeless and unchanging, but that's only because we aren't really looking carefully at them" ("Pamaltelan Equivalencies," Well of Daliath). No Stafford quote articulates the thinness as deliberate doctrine; the documented reasons are publication constraints and the wargame origin in Dragon Pass that gave Genertela its commercial gravity (Appelcline, "The Settings of Yore: Glorantha," *Designers & Dragons*, 2012-04-09).

The community read in 2018 framed the imbalance as productive, not as failure. Andrew Logan Montgomery's review of the *Glorantha Sourcebook* (2018): "focus allows the Sourcebook to lavish attention on this region"; "if you are looking to run a campaign in Pamaltela, this is not the resource for you" (andrewloganmontgomery.blogspot.com, 2018-03). The "Sense of Size II" essay on Well of Daliath observes that under-developed peripheries remain "better-defined than equivalent fantasy settings...better than most of Gondor in Middle Earth or much of Faerun once you get away from the Sword Coast."

### Tékumel — gesture by clause

M.A.R. Barker's *Empire of the Petal Throne* (TSR, 1975) and *Swords & Glory* (1984) author Tsolyánu in depth and leave the four rival Five Empires as one-clause anchors. The official tekumel.com "Lands" page (Tékumel Foundation): Yán Kór is "where the Baron áld plans his revenge upon the Tsolyáni who betrayed him"; Livyánu is "the land of Livyánu where the inhabitants worship the mysterious Shadow Gods"; Salarvyá is the empire that "hugs the fertile southern coast"; Mu'ugalavyá is named without a descriptive clause at all. About one-eighth of the planet's surface has published maps. The 1975 core rulebook collapses the four rivals to a single summary sentence (Slack review, andyslack.wordpress.com, 2012-04).

Barker's design philosophy on incompleteness is recorded in oral tradition rather than craft essay. Episode 9 of the *Hall of Blue Illumination* podcast (Tékumel Foundation): Barker "encouraged players to imagine different things that might exist on Tékumel"; the setting has "much of its history lost and mysterious" as feature. The trick the clauses do is specificity-without-elaboration: "Baron áld...revenge...betrayed" is a named actor, motive, and prior event in fourteen words. The reader supplies the court, army, and history.

### Greenwood's design-outward method

Ed Greenwood theorized the Forgotten Realms gesture in his Clarkesworld interview: "If I have a character say there are only two continents, I've shattered my development chances...but if the character just mutters something like, 'Not many other places open to us,' that's vague enough" (clarkesworldmagazine.com/greenwood_interview). His shorthand for a continent: "Over mountains to the north of Realm 2 is the land of Coronth, once a great empire but now a few nomadic, food-following tribes." Workflow: "I look at my immediate needs, make them dictate the geographical spots I'm going to super-detail and then start 'designing outwards from' until I cover the world."

The 1987 *Forgotten Realms Campaign Set* gestured at Kara-Tur, Maztica, and Zakhara through cartographic reservation — the maps stopped at the continent's eastern edge with no Cyclopedia of the Realms entry (Wikipedia, *Forgotten Realms Campaign Setting*; Appelcline product history). Greenwood's retroactive verdict on the fleshings (Candlekeep Q&A via Appelcline product history for *Kara-Tur: The Eastern Realms*, 1988): "the too-close-to-our-real-world additions like Maztica, the Hordelands, and Kara-Tur were a mistake in style." The 2021 tweet (x.com/TheEdVerse, 2021-12-20) confirms TSR's additions displaced Greenwood's original offstage kingdoms.

### Wolfe — the translator frame

Gene Wolfe's "A Note on the Translation" in *The Shadow of the Torturer* (1980) does work no other appendix in fantasy does: "in rendering this book — originally composed in a tongue that has not yet achieved existence — into English, I might easily have saved myself a great deal of labor by having recourse to invented terms; in no case have I done so...Such words as peltast, androgyn, and exultant are substitutions of this kind, and are intended to be suggestive rather than definitive." Every archaic noun in the tetralogy thereafter implies a real referent in the original document the reader cannot access. Peter Wright (*Attending Daedalus*, Liverpool UP, 2003) names the technique "world-building through absence": "by suggesting rather than explicating the world's depth, Wolfe engages readers as active interpreters reconstructing an implied universe." The lexicon at the back of *Citadel of the Autarch* (1983) is the documentation of what is gestured at, not what is authored — Andre-Driussi's *Lexicon Urthus* (1994, 2nd ed. 2008) catalogues hundreds of entries reconstructed from single mentions (the "Book of Exultant Families," referenced once, yields ~1,492 families by stated page count).

### Le Guin — knowledge framed as partial

Ursula K. Le Guin's "From Elfland to Poughkeepsie" (1973, in *The Language of the Night*, Putnam, 1979) names the test for whether a secondary world is real: change the proper names to mundane ones and read the passage again. If the language still reads as fantasy, the texture has done its work; if not, "a writer may use all the trappings of fantasy without ever actually imagining anything." *Always Coming Home* (1985) builds the meta-discipline of partial ethnography into the structure — the "Pandora" sections explicitly interrogate what the chronicler cannot know, and the Kesh epistemic principle ("our knowledge not close the circle, closing out the void, so that we forget that what we do not know remains boundless") is placed in the mouth of the fictional culture, making incompleteness a civilizational virtue rather than authorial limitation. *Tales from Earthsea* (2001) repeats the device in its foreword: "The way one does research into nonexistent history is to tell the story and find out what happened."

### Borges — the document as gesture

"Tlön, Uqbar, Orbis Tertius" (*Ficciones*, Sur, 1944) gestures by citation. The Uqbar entry in the encyclopedia (Volume XLVI of *The Anglo-American Cyclopaedia*) is described as a region "with an all-fantastic literature taking place in the mythical worlds of Mlejnas and Tlön" — three geographic referents, two fictional worlds, no further elaboration. The story does not describe Uqbar; it describes finding an entry about Uqbar. The two-step shifts trust to the encyclopedia, which presupposes editorial board, fact-checking, prior encyclopedias. The technique is en abîme nesting: Borges, then encyclopedia, then Uqbar, then Tlön, then Mlejnas within Tlön. Each layer implies further layers. The 1947 postscript names the stakes: a complete-enough fictional description colonizes reality. The Tlönian sample sentence "*hlör u fang axaxaxas mlö*" (rendered "upward, behind the onstreaming, it mooned") conveys an alien metaphysics in one phrase.

## Techniques that work

The mechanisms recur across the six sources, and they are not interchangeable.

The first is named specificity in one clause. "Baron áld plans his revenge upon the Tsolyáni who betrayed him" (Barker) and "out of Far Harad black men like half-trolls with white eyes and red tongues" (Tolkien) anchor the gesture on a concrete noun, leaving the reader to supply court, army, history. Greenwood's "the land of Coronth, once a great empire but now a few nomadic, food-following tribes" is the same move. An adjective without a noun does not gesture. "A mysterious land to the southwest" decorates. "The land of Livyánu where the inhabitants worship the mysterious Shadow Gods" gestures, because Shadow Gods is a noun the reader can hold.

The second mechanism is format. Borges's encyclopedia entry, Wolfe's translator's note, the Glorantha *Guide*'s gazetteer template — each carries an implicit promise of a complete version somewhere. Wolfe's "suggestive rather than definitive" turns gaps into evidence of source-material the reader cannot access. Borges's single found volume implies the other thirty-nine. The format is doing work that the prose alone cannot.

The third is incompleteness framed as epistemics rather than as authorial absence. Le Guin's Pandora cannot know everything, and the Kesh refuse to claim it. Stafford's later "hidden lands, unknown to all outsiders" retrofits Pamaltela's publication-constraint gap into in-world cosmography. Wolfe's translator cannot include what the original audience already knew. The reader does not register a gap; the reader registers a chronicler's horizon.

Restraint underwrites all three. Letter 247 names what the others practice: "to go there is to destroy the magic, unless new unattainable vistas are again revealed." Grybauskas's *A Sense of Tales Untold* reads Tolkien's whole achievement as knowing when to check the pen. The mechanism is asymmetric. Adding one fact narrows the world. Withholding it preserves the implication of an entire un-narrated history.

## Failure modes

M. John Harrison named the dominant failure mode in 2007: "Worldbuilding is the great clomping foot of nerdism. It is the attempt to exhaustively survey a place that isn't there...Worldbuilding numbs the reader's ability to fulfil their part of the bargain, because it believes that it has to do everything around here if anything is going to get done" (makingplaces.tumblr.com archive; cited via Manuel, "The Great Clomping Foot," *3 Quarks Daily*, 2018-02-26). Lincoln Michel ("Against Worldbuilding," *Electric Literature*, 2019-03-20) extends it into the distinction worldbuilding-vs-worldconjuring: "worldconjuring" uses "hints and literary magic to create the illusion of a world, with the reader working to fill in the gaps." G. M. Baker ("The Rise of Worldbuilding and the Decline of Literature," 2022-10) names the franchise version: properties going "world-forward rather than story-forward," with "lore" as the pejorative term for worldbuilding divorced from storytelling.

Posthumous Tolkien is the concrete case. *The Nature of Middle-earth* (ed. Hostetter, 2021) opens with six pages of tables on elf-population growth and offers, by reviewer consensus, "Tolkien just thinking on paper to himself, figuring things out rather than laying down sub-creational facts" (Krysta, *Pages Unbound*, 2022-03-26). Douglas C. Kane (*CS Lewis Journal* review): "unnecessarily redundant, confusing, and contradictory"; Hostetter "overstepping his role as editor" by presenting open questions as settled. Joseph Pearce (*The Imaginative Conservative*, 2020-03) argues for treating only lifetime-published works as canonical; posthumous material lacks "the authenticity which is inseparable from the authority of the author." The damage is structural: Letter 247 was Tolkien's prospective critique of his own posthumous publications. He foresaw it and was overruled by his son.

Star Wars Legends is the multi-author version of the same failure. Lucasfilm's 2014 decanonization rationale cited continuity load: "having 30+ years of canon material across different mediums can get a bit confusing" (Collider, 2014). TV Tropes catalogues the result on the "Continuity Snarl / Star Wars Legends" page — irreconcilable contradictions across authors each with their own interpretation of the universe. The mechanism is not the same as Tolkien's (multiple hands rather than over-explanation), but the symptom is identical: the corpus becomes ungovernable.

Forgotten Realms 3E. The fan critique is concise: readers preferred 1E "because the world is less defined...there is more to explore and find rather than having each and every square mile mapped out and every living NPC statted up down to their choice of favorite pastry" (Giant in the Playground, "Why do people hate the Forgotten Realms?"). The setting produced 34 sourcebooks across the 3E era. Greenwood's own "mistake in style" verdict is a parallel critique: real-world analogs imported via Kara-Tur and Maztica pulled gamers "out of roleplaying into disputes about historical details."

Sanderson Cosmere. John Ehrett (*Ad Fontes Journal*) frames the Cosmere as "a world of grand machines and processes, all churning on into eternity" — the magic systematized to the point that "the key question his stories pose is quintessentially modern: *how do we make things happen?*" Greg Camp (Medium, 2020-07) extends the diagnosis to the Wheel of Time scope: "as the Wheel of Time series drew on, it lost some of its feel, precisely because the sheer scope of the world and the number of viewpoints diminished the sense of wonder; readers started to learn how everything worked." The failure mode is hardness-mystery inversion: hardening one increases certainty and decreases wonder one-for-one.

Counter-cases exist. The *Silmarillion* (1977) strengthened LotR's allusions because the backstory was already complete in JRRT's mind during LotR's composition — the depth was real, then revealed; not invented to plug gaps. *The World of Ice and Fire* (2014) is read by some as deepening Yi Ti via Lovecraftian un-resolution (conflicting accounts, lost histories, unreliable narrators) rather than closing mystery (*Race for the Iron Throne*, 2014-11-07). The rule the counter-cases suggest: fleshing that multiplies mystery (adds further gestured-at depth) strengthens worlds; fleshing that resolves mystery (closes questions the reader had been invited to hold) weakens them.

The named failure-mode vocabulary, in order of usefulness for Alaria's linter and review prose:

- "Great clomping foot of nerdism" (Harrison, 2007) — the urge to survey exhaustively.
- "Worldbuilding vs worldconjuring" (Michel, *Electric Literature*, 2019) — exhaustive documentation vs suggestive hinting.
- "Continuity snarl" (TV Tropes) — the structural collapse of a corpus over-authored by too many hands.
- "Lore-forward, not story-forward" (Baker, Substack, 2022) — franchise variant.

## Recommendations for Alaria

### 1. Operationalize tier discipline by retiring the `weight` field as authoring signal and replacing it with linter-checked structure

The lore-style-guide already records that `weight` is set in 1 of 3,541 entity files. It is effectively unused as guidance. Two specific moves:

- Stop relying on `weight` as a length cap. Make `zoomLevel` plus `entityType` the de-facto signal, as the style guide already advises. The Foundations stage should not invest in retro-populating `weight` across 3,541 files.
- Replace "tier discipline" as authoring concept with "gesture discipline": every entity at footnote/minor scale must carry at least one outgoing edge in the `history`, `cosmology`, `culture`, or `origin` families. The edge is the gesture. No outgoing-upward edge = a stub that points at nothing.

This is the Barker test, ported. "Where the Baron áld plans his revenge" works because it names a *person*, a *prior event*, and an *ongoing motive*. In Alaria's graph this would be three edges: `polity/ruledBy` person-X, `history/participatedIn` event-Y, `polity/rivalOf` entity-Z. The clause in prose then condenses what the edges already assert.

### 2. Add a Linter check (Foundation #5) for missing-gesture stubs

`scripts/lint-stubs.mts` (proposed) flags any entity whose outgoing edges include none of the following kinds:

- `history/participatedIn`, `history/occurredDuring`, `history/caused`, `history/precededBy`
- `cosmology/sourceOf`, `cosmology/flipSideOf`, `cosmology/inhabits`, `cosmology/channels`
- `culture/worships`, `culture/originatedIn`
- `origin/foundedBy`, `origin/createdBy`, `origin/ruinsOf`, `origin/successorOf`
- `polity/atWarWith`, `polity/rivalOf`, `polity/vassalOf`
- `possession/imprisons`

The edge kinds above are all present in `.claude/rules/entity-relations.md`; the check requires no new vocabulary. The body-length signal does not drive the check — a thirty-word stub with one `history/occurredDuring` edge passes; a thousand-word entity with only `spatial/within` and `culture/inhabitedBy` fails. The absence of an *upward* edge is the failure, not the shortness of the prose.

The current style-guide note that "half are ≤15-word stubs" gives a population bound: roughly 1,750 entities are stubs. The linter should report the count of stubs failing the check, not block the build, until a remediation pass closes the gap. Output goes to a tracked CSV under `data/lint-reports/missing-gestures.csv`.

Counterpart check: tier inflation. Any entity with `weight: footnote` or `weight: minor` whose body exceeds ~800 words is a candidate for re-tiering or trimming. This is the "clomping foot" detector — it surfaces drift between authoring intent and prose volume.

### 3. Reject the proposed `gestureTarget` frontmatter field

The topic brief proposed considering a dedicated frontmatter field naming "what this stub points at." Reject that proposal. The existing edge vocabulary in `.claude/rules/entity-relations.md` already does the work — a `history/occurredDuring` edge to an era is the upward gesture, a `cosmology/sourceOf` edge to a daemon is the cosmogonic gesture, an `origin/ruinsOf` edge is the gesture at the lost thing. Adding a frontmatter field duplicates graph data and creates a second source of truth, violating the one-direction-only discipline already encoded in `entity-relations.md`. The lever belongs in the linter, not the schema. No change to `entity-relations.md` or the frontmatter spec in `.claude/rules/entity-files.md` is required.

### 4. Author one paragraph in `lore-style-guide.md` codifying the gesture rule

The style guide's "Length by importance" section currently leans on `zoomLevel` and `entityType` and stops there. Add a paragraph (proposed placement: after "Low-zoom entities — waypoints, minor water features...get 2-3 tight sentences and no section headers."):

> A short entry must still gesture. One concrete clause that names an actor, an event, or a cause — a war this town survived, a daemon whose name it keeps, a migration that emptied it — does the work of a paragraph. Tolkien's "out of Far Harad black men like half-trolls with white eyes and red tongues" (*Return of the King*, Book V Ch. VI) is the model: one clause, one named place, one image, no decoration. Vague atmospherics ("ancient secrets," "mysterious past," "long history") do not gesture; they decorate. Test the entry by asking whether a reader who saw only it would know one specific thing about the world beyond this entity. If not, the entry is filler, not a stub.

The depth-is-referential thesis in `plan/high-level.md` requires this discipline at the prose layer. The linter check in Recommendation #2 enforces it at the graph layer.

### 5. Set explicit ratio targets as the corpus scales 3,541 → 10,000

Pamaltela's ratio is the precedent, with caveat. *The Guide to Glorantha*'s ~300 of ~800 pages cover Genertela in depth; the remainder spreads across Pamaltela, mythic history, and reference. By page-density-per-region, Genertela runs roughly 5× Pamaltela. The page ratio does not translate cleanly to entity counts — a Glorantha region chapter may correspond to dozens of Alaria entities — so the page split is an upper-layer signal, not a per-entity quota. Forgotten Realms 3E sprawl is the explicit failure case at the entity layer (every village statted, per Giant in the Playground fan critique).

Recommended target shape for Alaria's gazetteer at 10,000 entities, derived from the Glorantha/Tolkien precedent, the depth-is-referential thesis in `plan/high-level.md`, and the existing observation that "half are ≤15-word stubs":

- Roughly 5–10% of place entities (500–1,000) carry multi-section authoring: nations, capitals, the Pincer Region's full corpus, regions of demonstrable historical weight.
- Roughly 15–25% (1,500–2,500) carry one-paragraph authoring with two or more live edges to history, cosmology, or origin — the towns and ruins that history actually touches.
- The remaining 65–80% (6,500–8,000) stay at one or two sentences plus at least one upward edge, forever. This is the Pamaltela tier. The corpus reads as deep because these stubs each name one specific thing, not because they are filled in.

The Pincer Region prototype (Foundation #6) should measure its own actual distribution after the loop completes and use that distribution to calibrate the ratio empirically before propagating to other regions.

### 6. Warn the authoring pipeline against three named failure modes

The current `plan/guidelines.md` and `lore-style-guide.md` do not name these. The recommended addition to `lore-style-guide.md` (in "Things to avoid"):

- The clomping foot. Authoring everything is the dominant failure mode in mature settings (Harrison, "the great clomping foot of nerdism," 2007; Forgotten Realms 3E sprawl per Greenwood). Each entity earns its length; default to fewer words.
- The notes-to-self trap. Tolkien's *The Nature of Middle-earth* (2021) shows what happens when an author publishes the working calculations rather than the finished gesture: elf-population spreadsheets read as machinery, not myth. The `<!-- author-notes -->` block exists precisely to keep this material out of public output. Use it.
- Explanation foreclosing mystery. Letter 247's "to go there is to destroy the magic, unless new unattainable vistas are again revealed" (Tolkien, *Letters*, 1981) is the rule. The `mysteries-must-have-answers` rule in `lore-style-guide.md` is sound — the corollary is that the answer is for the GM via the `<!-- author-notes -->` block, not for the public entry. Resolving a stub's mystery in public text collapses the gesture.

### 7. The scholar frame is structurally synergistic with the gesture model; document the synergy explicitly

`.claude/rules/scholar-attribution.md` already requires that attribution be approval-gated, and that coherent direct assertion is the default. The synergy is this: a stub authored as a scholar's brief mention — "Pembling notes only that the tower's foundations predate the Frost Fall" — is doubly cheap. The in-world scholar may not know more (epistemic limit), and the out-of-world author has not written more (authorial limit). The Wolfe translator frame is the operating model.

This is not a change to `scholar-attribution.md`'s rules; the rules already permit it. It is a change to the *examples* in the rule file. Add an example block illustrating the gesture-by-scholar move:

> A scholar mention can carry a gesture without committing the corpus to fact. Example:
> > *"The foundations predate the Frost Fall. What stood here before is no longer recorded."*
> > — Pembling of Grendenheim, *Survey of the Northern Marches*, 3,221 SD
>
> This authors the gesture (something older than the Frost Fall existed here) without authoring the referent. The scholar's epistemic limit and the author's restraint align.

This is an *example addition* to `scholar-attribution.md`, not a rule change. The rules — attribution gated by approval, coherent assertion as default — remain untouched.

### 8. Single most decision-relevant action for Foundations stage

Build the missing-gesture linter check (Recommendation #2) as part of Foundation #5. The check is cheap to implement against the existing edge graph and surfaces, in one report, the population of entities that read as threadbare under the depth-is-referential thesis. Without that count, the project cannot evaluate whether the depth-is-referential claim is actually holding across the 3,541-entity corpus, and cannot calibrate the 10,000-entity ratio in Recommendation #5. Every other recommendation here depends on knowing how many stubs currently gesture and how many do not.
