Alaria runs on deep time. The titans made the world more than twelve million years ago, the first humans woke barely five hundred thousand years past, and the sun overhead has been awake and lonely the whole while. Against numbers like that, a mortal life is a flicker. The figures in this section are the flickers that left a burn mark. They are not the most powerful beings in the world, as a rule. They are the ones whose choices the world is still living inside.

What it takes to matter here is rarely raw strength. The strongest things in Alaria are forces, not people: the sun's slow will, the leylines, the substrate of spent emotion the whole world floats in. A person of consequence is someone who turned one of those forces, or stood in front of it, or refused it at the right moment. Sometimes that is a queen with an army. More often it is a scholar who reconstructed a dead language, a smith who founded a clan-rite that outlasted the clan, a traitor whose single broken oath rerouted a war. The lever matters more than the muscle.

### The shapes a life of consequence takes

The figures who recur in Alaria's histories sort, loosely, into a handful of kinds. There are the founders, who made a thing that kept running after them: a city, a covenant, a craft tradition, a law. There are the conquerors, who redrew borders and are remembered in proportion to how much blood the redrawing cost. There are the sages, who did not act on the world so much as decide how it would be remembered, which in a world this old is its own kind of power. There are the traitors and the oathbreakers, who are named precisely because a culture needs a name to attach the wound to. And there are the deathless, who refused the ordinary exit and usually regretted it.

That last kind is worth a word, because Alaria makes the refusal of death unusually literal. Gino the Terrible bought himself eternal life and discovered the bill came due in the world around him; everything near him rots while it still lives, and so the heroes who could not kill him chained him in the White Wastes instead, where there was nothing left to corrupt. He is still there. Deathlessness in Alaria is rarely a triumph. It is a sentence the figure chose before they understood the terms.

> They could not kill him, so they took him north past the last well and the last grass, fixed the chain to the deep rock, and walked away while he was still cursing them. The cold does not rot. That was the whole of the mercy in it.
> — from a Wastes-warden's account of the binding of Gino

### How a name survives

A mortal of consequence has two deaths in Alaria, and the second one is the one that frightens people. The first is the body's. The second comes when the last person who holds your true name lets it go, because in this world a name held in memory is the thread that keeps the spirit from ending for good. This is not metaphor. It is the plain mechanics of death here, and it makes being remembered a form of survival rather than vanity. A king forgotten is a king ended, more completely than any assassin could manage.

So the people who shape Alaria fight for their names as hard as they fought for anything in life, and they do it through other people. The Iron Orc is remembered because the black orc tribes still tell how he tortured his own skin past the hardness of armor and finally stood in dragon-fire and did not burn; the telling is the monument, and it has outlasted every weapon he carried. The scholars are the other half of this. Erindath of Sennos and Pembling of Grendenheim never conquered a field or founded a hall, but the names that survive from their eras survive in large part because those two wrote them down, biases and all. To be recorded by a partisan is to be remembered through a distorting glass. It is still far better than not being remembered.

### The thin line

At the top of every list of consequential lives sits a possibility most of them never reach for and a few seize: a mortal can become a god. The deities of Alaria, the daemons, were every one of them mortals once, who died carrying the right kind of spirit and rose into something prayer could feed. The full machinery of that ascension, what it costs and who can pay it, belongs to the Pantheon. What belongs here is the shape of the temptation, because it bends mortal ambition the way nothing else does.

The line between a great mortal and a new god is thinner than it should be, and it is not always crossed by merit. Sometimes a god is simply manufactured. The silver elves did this to Tiira: they took a clever girl, schooled her into a vessel, cut away the parts of her past they did not want her keeping, and installed her as a deity their people were then required to worship. A consequential life can end with you on an altar as the worshipped rather than the worshipper, and not always by your own choosing.

It helps to hold two figures at the far edges, to see how rare the apex is and at what scale the truly large lives play out. Lyzaria, the first of the titans to die, chose her death and in choosing it carved the channels every later death in Alaria flows through; she is the reason there is such a thing as a remembered name at all. Gaea filled the world with flesh and feeling and spent herself so completely doing it that she is now diffused through the ground rather than standing on it. Neither is a person in the ordinary sense, and neither is the kind of figure this section is mostly about. They are the ceiling. Below it, in the half-million years since the first humans woke, run the mortals who changed something and got remembered for it: the founders and conquerors, the betrayers and the sages, the deathless in their chains, the rare few who rose. The histories that follow are mostly arguments about which of them deserve the room they take up.
