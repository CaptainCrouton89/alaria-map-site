### Power that outlives the powerful

Kings die, and their heirs quarrel, and a crown that looked permanent turns out to have been one lucky succession away from collapse the whole time. The bodies that actually move Alaria are older and harder to kill. A church keeps its doctrine when the bishop dies. A trade alliance keeps its routes when one member nation slides into civil war. A secret order keeps its purpose when every founding member is generations in the ground. If you want to understand why the world looks the way it does, do not start with the thrones. Start with the institutions that the thrones have to negotiate with.

This is the chapter on organized power: the churches and priesthoods, the trade alliances, the martial and knightly orders, the druidic circles, the arcane societies, and the cabals that prefer no one knows they exist at all. A faction in Alaria is any group that has learned to act as one thing across time and distance. That is harder than it sounds, and the ones that manage it are the ones worth knowing.

### Where the leverage comes from

Organized power in Alaria runs on four kinds of leverage, and most of the great factions hold more than one.

The first is faith, and faith here is not a metaphor. A daemon survives only so long as the living remember it and tithe to it, because Deoric magic spends life and prayer is the slow life-tithe that keeps a daemon's reserve above the floor where being forgotten would kill it. A church is therefore not only a moral institution. It is the supply line that keeps a god alive, and a god that is kept alive can spend that pooled life on miracles. The arrangement runs both directions. The Church of Margia, leading church of Hephake, can promise its faithful real intervention because its faithful are the thing that funds the intervention. Threaten a church's congregation and you are not threatening its dignity. You are threatening its god's heartbeat.

The second is wealth, which mostly means routes. Goods move along corridors, corridors can be opened or closed, and whoever sets the tariff at the chokepoint collects without ever having to grow or make a thing. The Aldriktch Trade Alliance is a standing agreement among six nations to keep the routes between them open and the tolls capped, which is worth more to its members than any single port they could seize by force. It is also, like every alliance, exactly as strong as its weakest member's patience.

> The Alliance does not love Myorna. The Alliance loves Myorna's silver, and tolerates Myorna so long as the silver moves. The day the silver stops, watch how quickly love is rediscovered for somebody else.
> — attributed to an Erasnus customs-master, on the strain in the trade coalition

The third is force, held by the orders that make a profession of arms or hunting. Some are sworn to a cause, like the Pity Knights who once hunted liches until one of their own became the thing they hunted. Some sell their skill to the highest bidder, like the Soldiers of the Third Eye, time-attuned mercenaries who can read a battle's outcome before it is fought and who pledge loyalty to no crown. An order of this kind is dangerous out of proportion to its size, because it concentrates skill that a levy of conscripts cannot match and carries it wherever it is paid to go.

The fourth is knowledge, the quietest leverage and often the most durable. A druidic circle that remembers which leylines still run and which have gone dead holds something no army can take by storm. An arcane society that guards a working its rivals have lost can sell that secret one fragment at a time, forever. The Druids, the elder beings the elves descend from, are powerful less for their numbers, which are few, than for what they have simply lived long enough to know.

### How they hold it, and how they lose it

A faction's first real problem is continuity. A king's authority transfers by blood or by sword in a single bloody afternoon; a faction's authority has to survive the slow turnover of every person inside it, which means it has to live in something other than a person. Churches live in doctrine and rite. Orders live in oaths and a chain of initiation. Trade bodies live in written charter and the mutual interest that signed it. When that connective tissue thins, the faction hollows out without anyone deciding to end it. The First Brotherhood, once the sworn bond that held every dwarf to the survival of dwarves, is now closer to a dues-collecting union with three city-states monopolizing its leadership. The oath is still recited. Fewer and fewer people act as though it binds them.

The second problem is that leverage attracts rivals who want the same leverage. Two churches whose gods claim the same domain are not merely theological competitors; they are fishing the same pool of worshippers, and every soul one of them wins is life-tithe the other's god does not receive. This is why so many pantheons in Alaria are really cartels: gods with carefully non-overlapping domains, agreeing not to compete because competition would starve them both. Break the agreement, expand into a partner's domain, and the cartel fractures into open religious war. The same logic runs through trade and arms. Wherever two factions need the same thing to live, they are enemies whether they have admitted it yet or not.

> We do not ask a sellsword which side he believes in. We ask which side has paid. The day you forget that distinction is the day you hand a stranger your throat and call it an alliance.
> — a maxim taught to young commanders of the eastern free companies

### Why the cabals matter most

The factions that announce themselves are the ones you can negotiate with, tax, excommunicate, or besiege. The dangerous ones are the factions that prefer you never learn their name. A cabal trades visibility for reach: by staying hidden it can place its people inside the institutions that do operate in the open, and steer them without ever holding a title. The Ivory Hand is the clearest example, an order that pulls strings across the world to keep one specific trade moving. It deals in slaves bought and sold to be sacrificed for Deoric workings, because a life taken in blood yields a lump sum of power that years of patient prayer cannot match. Its membership reaches into a queen's court, a city's merchant houses, and the confidence of dragons. No one elected it. No army can march on a thing whose borders it cannot find.

Not every hidden faction holds real power, and a GM should keep the difference straight. The Moonwatchers wait in scattered cells for a dead moon to return and remake the world, and most governments file them under harmless until one of their cells turns up beside a mass grave. A cult's threat is rarely its theology. It is what a small group of people who believe absolutely will do when they decide the time has finally come.

The throne in any given realm is real, and worth playing for. But the crown is usually answering to something. A church it cannot afford to anger. A trade body it cannot afford to lose. A debt to an order, sworn three generations back. A whisper from a hand it has never seen. The useful question to ask of any kingdom is not who wears the crown. It is who the crown has to keep happy.
