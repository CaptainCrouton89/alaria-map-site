Start with the picture every Alarian sky captain carries in their head. The world is not a globe hanging in empty space. It is a slab, an enormous flat shelf of stone and soil with an inhabited top and a far less hospitable bottom, and that slab floats in a sea. The sea is the Astral Plane, a true ocean of astral solution thick enough to be nearly opaque, and it is dotted with islands of buoyant stone that ride it the way icebergs ride water. The largest of those islands tower for miles and carry cities. The smallest break loose, plummet, and become the floating stones that the entire sky trade is built on. Everything else in Alarian cosmology is a matter of which direction you travel from that slab, and what you find when you arrive.

There are six planes in all, and the distinction between them matters. Four are true planes, full worlds in their own right: the Material Plane, the Astral Plane above, Malstaris below, and Celestia off to the side. The other two are overlays, the Ethereal and the Nethereal, thin sheets of plane laid across all the rest rather than worlds you could stand on. The Elemental Planes are not part of this count at all. They are a separate stack of nine layers pressed up against Alaria's own, reachable only along leylines where one of them bleeds close to the surface, which is exactly why Kethic elemental magic always feels like power borrowed from somewhere it was never meant to leave.

The Material Plane is the slab, and it has two faces. The upper face is Belagria, the inhabited world: the Surfacelands where nations quarrel over borders and harvests, and beneath them the Underrealms, a two-hundred-mile crust of tunnel and cave. Dig deep enough and your weight begins to fade, until you reach Emphathum, a stretch of weightless dark strung with drifting rock. Keep going and gravity returns the other way around. Now you are falling upward into Phlethageros, the slab's cruel underside, its maze and its lava-scoured Wastes. The demons of the Stalactites of Geth live down there in the heat, and below the Wastes the slab simply ends.

> The first man to fall all the way through and come back was a Tarkhetan miner named Velko, or so the leyline guilds tell it. He went down a Force fissure that opened under his quarry, drifted three days through Emphathum, and came up on the underside facing a sky that poured lava instead of rain. He did not stay. The guilds keep his name because he is the only one who is supposed to have made the descent on purpose and returned to argue about it.

The two halves of the slab explain why the Astral Plane reads as a "sea" and not a "sky." From Belagria you look up at it; from the lowlands of Phlethageros it is somewhere else entirely. Above the surface, the Astral Sea presses down close enough that its lowest currents dip into the open air a few thousand feet over the ground. Those currents are the highways of the sky trade. Ships do not sail to the true sea, which lies thousands of miles up through thickening solution. They hang from a fragment of fallen astral stone and ride the currents where the currents come to them, threading slow circuits between the trade cities while the great floating islands wheel far overhead, invisible behind the opaque water.

The other directions are stranger. Travel down past the Wastes, off the underside of the slab, and you arrive in Malstaris, a gray and emotionless expanse under the heavy light of Nydus, the Black Sun. Travel up far enough through the Astral Sea and you reach the Astral Plane proper, lit by Aurus, the White Sun. These two are a matched pair, the same axis pointing opposite ways. But there is a third move that has nothing to do with up or down. Walk far enough in any cardinal direction and you do not fall off the edge of the world. You wrap around it into Celestia, the Material Plane's mirror, and you arrive at the reflected counterpart of wherever you crossed. Celestia is shaped by what the living believe rather than by physical law, which is why a daemon who wants a palace there must convince mortals to build one here first, so its reflection can exist for them to enjoy.

How a person comes apart at death is the load-bearing fact of the whole structure, so it is worth stating plainly. Every Alarian is held together by three strands. The soul is borrowed light from Aurus, the shadow is borrowed dark from Nydus, and between them sits the spirit, the part made of Ezz that is actually you, your will and your character. When the binding threads break, the three go three ways. The soul rises to the Astral Plane and eventually cycles back through Aurus to be drawn out again for some later life. The shadow sinks to Malstaris and cycles likewise through Nydus. The spirit fades to Celestia, and here the symmetry stops cold. There is no reservoir for spirits and no return. A spirit lasts exactly as long as someone living still remembers its true name. When the last person who knew that name forgets it, the spirit does not dissolve into anything. It ends. The finality of death in Alaria is not the splitting of soul from shadow, which is routine and reversible across many lives. It is the forgetting that erases the self for good.

> Souls cycle. Shadows cycle. You do not.
> — a saying common to Alarian deathbed rites

This architecture was not always complete. Souls and shadows are older than Ezz, and the first two roads of death were carved by Lyzaria, the first titan to choose to die, whose soul became the substrate of the Astral Sea and whose shadow became the oldest power in Malstaris. For millions of years death had only those two strands. The third arrived with the Ezz Rift, when Melera's escaped music flooded the world with the thought-and-emotion substrate and condensed Celestia out of a long-dormant seed, making spirits possible for the first time. The spirit's road to Celestia was threaded into a framework that already existed. Lyzaria built the road; the Rift opened a third lane on it.

A handful of those spirits do more than wait to be forgotten. The rare one that held true command of Deoric in life can reach back across the boundary and act on the living world, and these are the daemons, worshipped as gods and sustained by the prayers that keep their names alive. Their economics, their hoarded grammar, and the long war that thinned their ranks belong to the Pantheon, the sibling page to this one; the planes are the rooms, and the gods are who you find living in them. What matters for the structure of reality is only that Celestia houses both the ordinary dead and these few who learned to answer prayers, and that none of them, god or not, outlasts the memory of their name.

Two cautions for anyone using this as a map. First, the threads themselves run through the Ethereal and Nethereal overlays, and no living thing can enter those and stay whole, because to step into your own binding is to step inside what holds you together. Diviners perceive the overlays; nobody walks them. Second, Alaria is not the only world. It is one planar stack among many in a wider cosmos, and the stacks are genuinely foreign to one another, with their own laws of magic and physics. Places like Glyssen and Klokas are not distant corners of Alaria but other stacks entirely, reached and described by the rare traveler who came back able to describe anything at all. Within Alaria's own six planes the rules above hold without exception. Past its edges, they stop being reliable.
