An artifact is an object that does something no object should. The line between a very good sword and an artifact is not sharpness or age or the name of the smith. It is that power has been put inside the thing and stays there, working, whether or not anyone present understands how. A king who cannot read a single Deoric rune can still draw a blade whose runes cut through plate, because the runes do the work and he only has to swing. That gap between what an object can do and what its holder comprehends is the whole danger of artifacts, and it is why so few of them are loose in the world.

Power does not bind into matter cheaply. Alaria's four sources of magic mostly pass through a caster and are gone, and the two that linger in objects, Deoric and the soul-work that the enchanting traditions call ensouling, both demand something permanent in exchange. Deoric is the titans' command-language, and it costs life to speak. Inscribe it on most materials and the charge bleeds out as the medium weathers; only titan blood and titan bone hold a rune durably, which is why a true charged object is almost always built on a scrap of the dead titans and why such scraps are fought over. Ensouling is stranger and arguably worse. To make a mechanism run without fuel, you seat a soul in it, and the soul keeps the wheels turning the way a soul keeps a body breathing.

> The Golden Enchanting Table runs on souls. Lesser ones drive the gears; a single legendary soul sits at its core and powers every rune the table offers. No one who studies the table likes to dwell on where those souls came from, only on what the gears produce when the runes on their teeth drift into alignment.

This is the trade that makes the codex file artifacts under an ancient and uneasy heading rather than a triumphant one. To bind power into a thing is to bind a piece of something living, or a piece of the dead world itself, into it for good. The Ezzal Artifacts skip the binding step entirely, because they are matter that was already soaked in raw Ezz during the Rift twelve million years ago and never let go of it. The first dragons hoarded that material; a dwarven king later took it and had it forged into the thought-swords, which is the kind of provenance most powerful objects carry. Made by one hand, taken by another, used by a third who never asked the first two how it worked.

A handful of artifacts hold their power through no binding at all. The Four Dragon Blades are not enchanted, carry no rune, draw on no soul. A dark elf master smith simply worked rare material with skill that has not been matched since, and the blades came out mythic, swinging faster than their crude shape should allow and never going dull. Pure craft can reach the same place as magic if the maker is good enough and the material rare enough. A thing made that way is also harder to unmake. There is no rune to scratch out, no soul to release, only the object itself, which is why the blades keep turning up in new hands generation after generation.

Then there are the objects that no one made. The Labyrinth beneath the Evertorch cathedral is not built; it is the shape a Time leyline takes where it surfaces, a structure whose corridors are not the same length on the way back and whose lamps burn cold. The codex files it among artifacts because it behaves like one, holding and dispensing a power that does not belong to ordinary matter. But it answers to the world's deep structure rather than to a craftsman, and that is the most dangerous kind of all. A made artifact can in principle be understood by studying its maker. The Labyrinth has no maker to study.

> Every fool who covets one of these thinks he is buying power. He is buying a debt someone else ran up. The thing was paid for in full when it was made. All he has bought is the right to carry it for a while.
> — attributed to an unnamed enchanter of the nine-ringed city, quoted often and sourced never

The artifacts catalogued here are few, and that is honest to the world. Most attempts to bind power into matter fail outright. The ones that succeed tend to cost more than the maker survived to enjoy, or they outlast their keepers and drift down the centuries from owner to owner, scattered, re-found, and coveted by people who know what the thing does and not what it is. The short list that follows is what is left after all the failures. Each entry on it has gathered a history the way a blade gathers notches.
