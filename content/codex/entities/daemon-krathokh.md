**Domains:** The anchor-vessel rite as divine invocation; the moment of binding a daemon to a living conduit; the priestly act itself, distinct from the priestly technology.

**Era of ascension:** Golden Age of Man (~145,000 years ago). **Cohort: dead.** **Died:** God War & Long Winter (~75,000 years ago), when the God War shattered the anchor-vessel priesthood and the act that constituted his worship ceased being performed across the world simultaneously. His death is the structural keystone the rest of the Golden-Age pantheon's deaths rested on.

**Worshipped by:** The anchor-vessel priesthoods of the Golden Age — the trained institutional class who performed the binding rite to install daemons into living conduits, and whose work, across cultures, was the load-bearing infrastructure of every Golden-Age cult's continued material presence. No living culture maintains his worship. (The worships edge is authored downstream on the worshipper, not here.)

### Overview

The anchor-vessel was the priestly technology — a vessel (sometimes a person, sometimes a constructed reliquary, sometimes a temple itself) prepared to hold a daemon's presence on the material plane. Krathokh was not the vessel. He was the *daemon of the rite that invoked the technology*, the divinified act of binding rather than the bound thing. The doctrinal distinction was load-bearing across the Golden Age pantheon: every other daemon's continued material reach depended on the regular performance of the rite Krathokh embodied, and the priesthoods who performed it across cultures were therefore the structural class without which the whole pantheon would lose its hold.

His cult understood itself in exactly this way. The anchor-vessel priesthoods of the Golden Age were not the priests of Krathokh in the populist sense — they were tiny, specialist, internally hierarchical orders that maintained the rite for all the *other* cults, which paid them and supported them and treated them with the wary respect due to the people on whom every other priesthood depended. The doctrine was meta-religious. A priest of Aedorath could keep the harvest rite; only a priest of Krathokh could bind Aedorath into the vessel that made her continued presence at the rite possible. Without him, the harvest priest was praying at an empty altar.

### Fall

The God War broke the priesthoods directly. The first decade saw inter-faction violence target the anchor-vessel orders specifically — every faction grasped that the rival cults' continued material presence ran through Krathokh's people, and every faction therefore tried to capture or destroy the relevant priesthoods. The orders were small, geographically distributed, and slow to replace — formation took thirty to forty years — and inside a single decade they were operating at fractional capacity across the world. Vessels that needed re-binding went un-re-bound. Vessels that broke could not be replaced. The other daemons' material reach attenuated visibly in the years before the eruption, in measurable degradation of the rites that depended on the technology.

Krathokh died in this attenuation. The rite that constituted his worship was no longer being performed at the cumulative volume his god-body required, and his presence on the plane thinned correspondingly, even before any of his individual priests were killed. The texts describe his absence from the rites in the late pre-eruption years as a gradual fade rather than a discrete death — the bindings simply began not taking, the way a coal gives less heat as it cools. The eruption killed what was left of the orders directly; the cold finished the rest.

His death is the structural reason the Long Winter killed the Golden Age pantheon so completely. The anchor-vessel rite had been the institutional substrate for every other daemon's continued material existence; when the rite stopped, every cult was on attenuation timer regardless of whether the cold had reached its priests yet. The other gods died on their own timetables — Vassanéth in days, Henélleth in weeks, Aedorath in two harvests, Vrokún in three generations — but they died because the rite that anchored them had failed first. He is the keystone the entire collapse can be diagrammed against.

No successor daemon ever covered his domain. Mernath later partially organized the *memory* of the rite; nobody ever performed it again at world-altering scale.
