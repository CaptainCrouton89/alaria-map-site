A Whiteling is not bred. None is born to another Whiteling, and no two share a bloodline. One appears perhaps once in a generation, in the line of some ordinary gnome family that has never carried the trait and never will again. The family's first warning is usually a death in the house.

The cause sits a long way back. Deoric is the language of absolutes: a thing said in it, with sacrifice enough behind the saying, must occur. When a dark mage's working is cut short, the command does not fail with the mage. Deoric cannot fail; that is the whole of what it is. The absolute has been spoken and left unsatisfied, and an unsatisfied absolute is a debt reality is bound to pay. The debt waits. It can wait centuries. Then it finds flesh pliant enough to take the shape of a sentence still trying to finish itself, and gnomish stock, porous to raw structure where larger races are dense to it, is the flesh it finds. A Whiteling is the back half of a dead stranger's curse, born wearing a body.

This is why they see forward. The fate-sight is the command reading the years ahead for the object that would complete it, the verb hunting its missing noun across time. Their alabaster skin and parchment frames are thrift, not affliction. The body is the cheap part, run up quickly around something that was never meant to wear one, and it shows. No Whiteling has ever held its charge cleanly; the command in them leaks at the seams, because flesh was never built to hold an absolute the way titan bone can.

The infants are the danger. A newborn Whiteling cannot speak and cannot choose, but the absolute inside it completes stray scraps of whatever passes nearby. A word the mother says in exhaustion becomes literally and bindingly true. Deoric costs life, and the cost comes out of whoever stands closest. A father mutters that he would give anything for the crying to stop; the crying stops, and he is dead on the nursery floor with the price paid in full. Families that recognize the signs, the colorless eyes and the way flame gutters wrong in the room, smother the child in its first hours. They do it because the alternative is the household dying by accident, sentence by sentence, in its own offhand words. Most Whitelings die this way, unnamed.

The ones who live grow into a single discipline: keeping the sentence from finishing. Completion means discharge, all of it at once, and no one in the room survives that. Death is the worst trigger of all, because a dying vessel fails and lets the whole command go off whole. So the strong ones refuse to die. A Whiteling who has learned enough of its own grammar can speak the absolute of its own continuance, paying its death as the sacrifice and binding the sentence open forever. It rises as a lich. These are the finest Deoric workers in the world outside the daemons, for a reason no academy can copy: they never learned the language, they are the language. A Whiteling lich is an absolute that refused, against the nature of absolutes, to resolve. There is little the world fears more than a thing it made and cannot finish.

Every polity that understands what they are keeps a policy ready. Most settle on death in the cradle; a few keep a living Whiteling behind a sealed door and a use, and pay for the keeping. A free adult is hunted from one coast to the other, and the hunters are seldom wrong to bother. The few who survive without turning sometimes seek the rite of Qhytheryx, the daemon of the called voice. Where the Qipi call into the sinkhole and read what comes back, a Whiteling calls into the gap between what it has seen and what it has not, listening for the shape of its own missing word. The daemon answers. Whether the answer is the word or only an echo of the asking, the Whiteling can rarely tell until it has already come true.
