The world is Alaria. The part of it people live on is a single great slab of land and water lit by a sun that moves because it wants to. Cartographers call the surface the Surfacelands, and the two-hundred-mile crust of tunnels beneath it the Underrealms; together they make Belagria, the upper half of the Material Plane. The Atlas is concerned with that surface. What lies above it in the open sky, and what waits below the Underrealms where the world flips over and gravity runs the other way, are their own subjects. Here we keep to the ground a traveler can walk.

Start with the light, because everything else follows from it. Bryn, the sun, is an awakened thing, and it goes where the faithful sing it. There is no tilt to the world, no orbit that returns the warmth on a schedule. Congregations hold Bryn on a north or south trail by the labor of their voices, and the band of land it warms holds the bulk of the world's people, its farms, and its cities. Push far enough from that band, toward either pole, and you reach latitudes no coalition has ever held the sun across for long. The cold there is not a season. It is the permanent condition of a place the light does not visit, and it shapes the edges of the inhabited map more than any coastline does.

> A southern league grows its grain on a forty-day summer and a forty-day winter, and the priests on the warm side and the priests on the cold side hate each other with a patience you only see in people who have inherited the argument.

Most of the land sits in the warm middle, and most of that is gathered toward the center and west of the world. Rimihuica runs down the middle of it, a study in extremes: desert badlands in the north, lethal jungle and cloud-wrapped mountains through the waist, and the lawless Shacklands at its southern foot, where the slave markets and pirate captains answer to no crown. South and west of it the land breaks into two more great masses. Upoceax stacks itself in bands from a scorching northern desert down through ancient elven kingdoms and a mountain-ringed giant heartland to the polar ice at its southern tip. Aboyinzu hooks along the southwestern rim, temperate in its northwest, savanna and rainforest through its body, and cold mist along the Dragon's Spine in the far south. These three sit close enough that the seas between them are crossings rather than voids, and the powers along their shared waters spend a great deal of effort deciding who may use those crossings.

![A harbor town of the warm band, terraced into a headland above a working bay — the kind of place the sun is sung to stay over.](https://pub-2f7d72a936214040b067e1f9ffc82e63.r2.dev/images/overview-geography/illo-warmlands.webp)

The north has its own character. Clueanda forms the frozen crown of the northwest, where twin mountain ranges fence the polar tundra off from a softer southern coast. Its Middle Sea is temperate enough to carry busy trade between city-states, and that water matters out of all proportion to its size, because reaching it from the western oceans means passing a single strait. The Tarkhon Empire holds that strait and taxes everything through it. A chokepoint like that does more to organize a region's politics than any quantity of good farmland.

> The Tarkhon toll book, opened to a single page. Three columns: cargo, origin, duty owed. The duty column has been revised in four different hands over two centuries, each scratching out the last and writing higher. No vessel sails the strait without an entry. The book is the empire's true border, and it is made of paper.

Then there is the water, which is most of the world. The seas carry the bulk of its trade and a fair share of its danger. Some are crossings. Others are barriers that look like crossings, which is worse. The Sea of Fools east of Ve glitters with coral and inviting shallows, and nearly everything in it carries a slow poison, so its beauty is the trap rather than the reward. Stretches of open ocean run wide enough that the lands across them have grown into separate worlds. Ve, far to the east, is one such: a whole continent of savanna in the north and primordial jungle in the south, kept apart by so much water that its beasts and its peoples developed along lines the rest of the world never touched.

The islands are their own answer to all that water. The Western Isles scatter across the Shattered Sea at the western edge of the known world, a maze of atolls and shoals that no great power has ever fully held, and so they fill with independent states, pirate havens, and island cultures that prefer the arrangement. The Greenwater Isles sit warm and tropical between Upoceax and Rimihuica, their reefs and jungles a different kind of frontier. Archipelagos like these are where the map stops being a matter of borders and becomes a matter of who knows the channels.

Beyond all of it, the map runs out. West past the Western Isles the ocean goes deep and empty, and that emptiness is honestly drawn: it is the edge of what anyone has charted and returned from. The frozen latitudes hold a stranger frontier. Beneath the glaciers of the far north and far south lie the ruins of cities that stood under open sun in older eras, when some forgotten coalition dragged Bryn's path far enough to warm the poles for a generation or two. When those windows closed, the warmth did not come back, and the cities froze where they stood, grain still sealed in their jars. So the wild on Alaria is not only the unmapped distance west of the isles. Some of it is the wreckage of old civilization, locked in ice that the sun no longer reaches, and a determined enough expedition can go crack one of those cities open and walk its streets.
