Magic in Alaria has four sources, and almost everything a mortal can do with power traces back to one of them. The four sources do not blend, do not substitute for one another, and are not arranged on a ladder from weak to strong. They are simply different in kind, the way fire and water are different, and a practitioner trained in one usually cannot touch the others at all. What this rules out is a single practitioner drawing on two sources at once; two separate practitioners can each bring a different art to bear on one working, and the result carries the marks of both without either source having crossed into the other. Understanding any spell, miracle, song, or rune in this world starts with knowing which of the four it draws on, because that single fact tells you where the power comes from, what it costs, and who is going to end up paying.

The deepest of the four is Deoric, the command-language the titans spoke when reality was new. Deoric does not persuade the world or coax it; it states what will be, and if there is power enough behind the words, the world obeys. The catch is the price. Deoric runs on life, and it does not run cheap. The spoken language is all but dead now, too dangerous and too demanding for any living throat to carry far, but the written form survives in two forms of practice. Handmages tattoo Deoric runes onto the backs of their hands in titan blood, then articulate them through precise gestures; the runes draw on the astral and malstaric threads that bind soul, spirit, and shadow, and a hand that bears too many runes risks tearing those threads and killing its owner outright. Ritualists work the older, bloodier road, speaking Deoric over a sacrifice and spending a life to fuel the working. Both depend on titan blood and bone, the only substances that hold a Deoric charge without crumbling, which is why a handmage carries a small fortune in their own skin and why, in the wrong towns, that fortune gets people murdered for it.

> A handmage from Deo Esari, gloves off in a market stall to count coin. The runes on the backs of her hands are old scar tissue gone copper-dark, raised where the titan-blood ink sits under the skin. She keeps the right hand half-curled the whole time, never letting the fingers fall into the articulation that would wake the third rune. Habit. A careless hand has killed more handmages than any rival ever did.

The other three sources draw on Ezz, the substrate that flooded the world at the Ezz Rift and gave it spirit and emotion both. Ezz has two faces, thought and emotion, and two of the four sources are nothing more than those two faces worked directly. The Psywinds are the thought-face: the swirling mass of every mind's ideas and intentions, blowing through all of Alaria at once. Psy mages read those currents, push their own thoughts into them, and reach across distance to touch another mind. It is the same medium a daemon listens through to hear its name prayed and the same medium that carries a true name to every corner of the world the instant it is spoken. Faesong is the emotion-face, and it does not come from mortal minds at all. It is the ambient music of Melera, the imprisoned goddess, leaking out through the bars of her cage as a melody most creatures never consciously hear. Fae work it natively. Druids learn to attune to it. Mortal bards can tune to it and reproduce its fragments, and a room undone by a bard's song is often hearing Faesong without knowing the name. Gaeic Melodies, the most widely practiced bardic tradition, draw on a different and older source: the residual echo of Gaea's own creation-song, not Melera's ongoing music, though the two were long confused because both move through emotion.

![A bard playing a Gaeic Melody — an echo of Gaea's ancient creation-song still reverberating in living things long after the making was done — and the room turned to tears without quite knowing why; mortal ears hear the emotion but rarely trace it back to the Earth Mother.](https://pub-2f7d72a936214040b067e1f9ffc82e63.r2.dev/images/overview-magic/illo-faesong-bard.webp)

The fourth source confuses people, because it also runs on emotion. Kethic is the caster's own emotion, gathered and aimed and routed out through the Elemental Planes by way of the leylines that thread the material world. The distinction worth holding onto is simple: Faesong is Melera's emotion, heard from outside; Kethic is your emotion, directed from inside. The elemental schools are all Kethic, every fire-shaper and stone-singer and storm-caller among them borrowing one of the nine elements as a medium while drawing on their own emotional energy to do it. That energy replenishes naturally; the cost of Kethic at mortal scales is not a depleting reserve. The cost that matters is cosmological. Gaea herself worked Kethic to make the flesh-races, and she spent so much of herself across so many ages of creation that she is now diffused into her own children, present as a resonance in stone and root rather than a will that answers.

So the four costs run plainly. Deoric costs life, taken from the caster's threads or from a sacrifice on the altar. Kethic draws on the caster's own emotional energy, which replenishes naturally at mortal scales. The cost is cosmological: the Earth Mother's own diffusion across twelve million years of making. The Psywinds and Faesong cost nothing to the world, because the world is already full of them; what they cost the practitioner is sanity and skill, the long discipline of hearing a flood of thought or a melody beneath hearing without drowning in it. A psy mage who reaches too wide and hears the thoughts of thousands at once can be shattered by them. There is no free magic in Alaria. There is only the question of who is billed, and when.

> Every working is a withdrawal. The mage who tells you otherwise has simply arranged for someone else to be at the counter when the note comes due.
> — attributed to a Vyanoweir lecturer on Deoric grammar

This is also the engine under the gods. Daemons are mortals who carried a deep command of Deoric into death and now project it across the boundary from Celestia, where the plane's saturation with Ezz keeps them from speaking Deoric directly. A daemon cannot cast into Celestia, so it reaches across to a worshipper already mid-ritual on the Material Plane, correcting and completing a working the mortal began. Prayer pays for this. Every prayer is a small tithe of life flowing into the daemon's reserve, and that reserve has to stay above a remembrance-floor or the daemon, forgotten, simply ends. Miracles spend the reserve down; a saved city or a single resurrection costs the equivalent of years of one believer's prayers, and a world-altering act costs the prayers of a city across generations. The God War, when the old pantheon died, was a run of mass deficit-spending: gods withdrawing more life than their dwindling congregations could pay in, collapsing one after another like banks in a panic.

A handful of practices sit at the edges and are worth naming so a newcomer is not blindsided. The School of Summoning, on the contested border between Chechol and Turbuni, teaches communion and planar contact rather than any single source, and has spent two thousand years accumulating secrets it no longer fully understands. Aether is the other exception worth naming. It is not one of the four sources, but it is not mundane matter either; it is an exotic meta-fluid that rained down on Alaria after an eruption on the neighboring stack of Lanthornia, and it powers sky-ships and engines through a working only the aether-tappers can perform. Pulling it from the air and fixing it into fuel is no reproducible chemistry but an exotic planar craft, and it has failed in every hand but the goblins', which is the whole reason a continent's sky-trade hangs on a fuel the clans alone can make. These are the exceptions, and a curious reader will find more of them. The spine holds for nearly everything else. Name the source, and you already know most of what matters about the magic: where its power comes from, and who is going to pay for it.
