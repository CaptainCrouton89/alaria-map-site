Alarian history runs in eons, and every eon so far has ended the same way. A long age builds something, the building reaches some height it cannot hold, and then a single reality-warping event tears it down and starts the count over. There have been three such eons. The world is presently in the third, and the third is young. Read the whole span at once and the pattern is hard to miss: the catastrophes are not interruptions of the story, they are the joints the story turns on, and a great deal of what a traveler can dig up out of the ground today is the wreckage one of them left behind.

> Three times the world has been made, and three times unmade. We are the fourth-making, and we keep our records in ink that runs.
> — a saying common among the archivists of the Modern Era

The first eon is the Early Times, and it begins before Alaria exists at all. Azus and Melera were the first two gods, one holding that everything must be ordered and described, the other that everything was an instrument to be played. Together they made Ezz, the substrate that is equal parts thought and emotion. Then they fell out. Azus judged that Melera's chaos tainted any order he tried to build, so he sealed her in a cage of specificity and set about sorting the cosmos alone, drawing the planar stacks into neat arrangements over the better part of a billion years. Alaria was one of the last of those stacks to form, roughly 23.5 million years ago, and it opened almost empty: three planes, no spirit, no emotion, no Ezz. Into that quiet came the first beings, the titan-gods Aurus, Nydus, and Celest, and after them the titans, who built their great civilizations in the Astral Sea and the deep caves of Malstaris and reasoned in pure Deoric, with will but no feeling. One of them, Lyzaria, chose to die. Her death carved the first two channels of mortality, soul to the Astral and shadow to Malstaris, and every death since has flowed through the grooves she cut.

The Age of Titans ended the way every age ends here. Melera, working from her prison, sent out a thin ripple of music to call for help, and it was enough to crack the Alarian stack and let Ezz flood in. Emotion arrived in a world that had never felt any, and the titans, ageless and unprepared, went mad and killed themselves in droves or fled to the cosmos. What they left behind matters more than they did. The flood woke spirit and feeling as forces in the world, condensed into the seed Celest had hidden and bloomed it into the plane of Celestia, threaded a third strand into death, and gave rise to Gaea, the Earth Mother, who would fill Alaria with life. This is the start of the second eon, the Gaeaic Eon, and the Ezz Rift that opened it is the hinge the entire living world hangs on.

Gaea's long reign carried the world through most of the second eon. She made the beasts and bore three sons, the first Dragon, Wolf, and Lion, and for some eight million years their descendants ruled under what is remembered as the Reign of Dragons. The druids, ancient even then, made the elves out of branches and river-mud, and the elves walked the world alongside the dragons. The age held until one of Gaea's own creatures reached too far: the dragon Eyachria grew until cities stood on her back, then challenged Gaea for the keeping of Alaria, and her own father Nagatayora killed her for it. Her spine is the mountain range that still runs down the south of Aboyinzu. Then came humanity. Gaea made the flesh-races last, and humans proved the most ambitious of all of them, spreading across Alaria and raising the Golden Age of Man, a hundred and twenty-five thousand years of civilization that has never been equaled since. Flying machines crossed between the planes. Magical knowledge was common as bread.

> A grain-jar sealed in a city under the southern ice, cracked open by an expedition three eons later. The grain inside is still whole. The script glazed around the rim is Golden Age work, and no living scholar can read more than every third word of it. The people who packed that jar expected to come back for it.

The Golden Age ended in the catastrophe whose wreckage the present world cannot stop tripping over. Late in the age, mortals learned to cross into Celestia and began doing so by the million, anchoring their souls back on the material side so they could return. Among them, the Vyanoweir reconstructed enough of the dead titan-language to carry a true command of Deoric across their own deaths, and where the living kept their names, they woke in Celestia as the first daemons. Then the bond between the planes turned lethal. The God War broke out as failing daemons fought for the prayers that kept them alive, their expenditure of power shattered the anchors that let the dead return, and the war closed when a single zealot forced the eruption of the Kajiit volcano and dropped the world into a multi-millennia winter. The entire Golden Age pantheon died of forgotten names. From the ice rose Craggus, the first daemon of the reborn world, who pushed the glaciers back across fifteen thousand years and reigned as the one god of men. He did not survive his own age either. The Laughing Plague ended it, and the same plague birthed the dwarves out of those few who survived its madness changed.

What followed was the Lost Ages, the longest dark stretch in the record and the one the present world knows least about, because so little of it was ever written down or survived being written. A false god, a curse that made magic kill its caster, a five-hundred-year night under a shadowrift, tyrant dynasties broken by a killing moon, a final fratricidal war that left no winner. The world was already in ruins when the second eon's reckoning arrived. Hykravones, the Gray Prince, was a titan who had gone into the spaces between dimensions before the Ezz Rift and returned, millions of years later, to find everything he knew destroyed. He spent a single catastrophic year dismantling what remained. Nearly every daemon died with its worshippers; civilization fell thousands of years backward; and from the blood of Gaea's slain children rose the Sharabha, the Ulvsjael, and the Naga. Then the Gray Prince went to ground and slept, and legend holds he sleeps there still, somewhere underground, meaning one day to wake and finish the work. His Shattering is where the second eon ends and the third begins.

The third eon is the Modern Era, and it is the one the living world still stands inside. It opened with the Great Expansion, ten thousand years of survivors rebuilding from almost nothing, weathering the Frost Fall, learning to live with dragons creeping back into prominence. That long recovery closed at the World Fire around 10 BSD, a catastrophe still argued over but agreed to have reset the calendar; it is the zero that the Before and After of the present dating system count out from. The age since is the Seventh Dawn, now in its 3,376th year. Its opening centuries were the Dark Ages, when the Titansworn, cultists trying to wake Hykravones to save a world they were sure would burn again, had to be put down by war before anything stable could grow. Out of that came the political world of the present: the Tarkhon Empire taxing the strait it founded its capital to command, the Neferati who carried sacred fire north some six centuries ago and lost the throne they built, the prayer-coalitions that fight over where the sun walks and the Solar Accord they signed to stop killing each other over the harvest. The catastrophe that ends this eon has not arrived. Given the shape of the other two, the more careful among Alaria's record-keepers do not assume it never will.
